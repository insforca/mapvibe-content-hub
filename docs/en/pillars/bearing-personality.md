# Bearing = Personality

North-up is a convention. Not a rule.

When you rotate your map — 30°, 45°, 90°, even 180° — something shifts. The city you thought you knew looks new. Streets reveal geometry you never noticed. The neighbourhood you grew up in suddenly has a different energy on the page.

**Your bearing is a personality choice.** It says something about how you see the place.

---

## What Different Bearings Feel Like

::: tabs
**0° — Classic North-Up**
Familiar. Legible. The default. If you want the map to be immediately recognisable, this is it. Works well for cities with strong north-south grid logic (Manhattan, Chicago).
---
**30°–45° — The Diagonal**
The most surprising. Streets that run parallel become dynamic diagonals. Dense urban fabric gets a graphic quality that flat north-up loses. Often the most *beautiful* bearing for neighbourhoods.
---
**90° — East-Up**
Rivers run differently. The coast appears on a different side. Cities you thought you knew feel like they've been seen for the first time.
---
**180° — South-Up**
A deliberate provocation. The world flipped. Used by cartographers for decades as a statement. On a wall, it's a conversation starter — and it's *yours*.
:::

---

## How to Find Your Bearing

There's no formula. Drag the rotation slider and watch what happens to your streets.

::: tip
For diagonal city grids (Caracas, Buenos Aires, parts of São Paulo), a 30–45° bearing can make the geometric pattern of the streets leap off the page.
:::

For cities built on a river or coast, try aligning the water horizontally — whatever bearing that requires. The result is almost always striking.
