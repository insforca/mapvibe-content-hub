# What Your Bearing Says About You

Nobody chooses north-up because it means something. They choose it because it's the default — the way maps have always been printed, the way cartographers aligned their instruments, the way atlases were bound.

But a custom map isn't an atlas. It's a personal object. Which means the angle is a decision, and decisions reveal things.

---

## 0° — North-Up: The Pragmatist

You ordered north-up and there's nothing wrong with that.

It's clean. It communicates. It matches every other map you've seen of that city. When you point to a neighbourhood and say "I used to live there," nobody has to rotate their head to follow.

North-up is the map that works in a meeting room. It's legible, it's respectful of convention, and it's genuinely handsome when the city grid happens to align with the cardinal axes — think Medellín's Aburrá Valley running cleanly north-south, or Buenos Aires laid square to the Río de la Plata.

If you picked 0°: you know what you want and you're not performing originality. That's a personality type.

---

## 30° — The Sweet Spot

Something interesting happens between 15° and 45°: street grids that looked like a ruled notebook suddenly become a composition.

30° is the bearing that expats most commonly arrive at after experimenting. It's not radical enough to feel contrarian, but it's tilted enough to make diagonal streets look like they're going somewhere — leaning into the frame rather than running parallel to the edge.

It's also the bearing where natural features stop fighting the geometry. A river that ran flat across the bottom now cuts diagonally. A hillside neighbourhood that felt like it was falling off the edge of the frame now anchors the composition.

30° is the angle of someone who has thought about it.

## 45° — The Maximalist

45° is a commitment.

On cities with strict orthogonal grids — La Plata, Chicago, Medellín's downtown, São Paulo's central districts — 45° turns the street geometry into something that looks almost textile. The grid becomes a diamond pattern, visually dense, assertive, impossible to ignore.

This is the bearing for people who want the print to command attention from across the room. It works especially well in high-contrast themes: Vintage Noir, Neon Night, or the clean edge of Big Apple Energy.

If you chose 45°: you bought the print to be the first thing people ask about.

---

## 90° and Beyond — The Locals

There's a moment when you've lived somewhere long enough that the official orientation of the map stops making sense to you.

You walked that main drag thousands of times, and it was never running north. It ran *towards the sea* or *up the hill* or *away from the airport*. The city in your memory is oriented by landmarks and feelings, not cardinal directions.

Some people arrive at 90° — true east-up — and recognise it as the orientation of their commute: the way they always mentally drew the neighbourhood when giving directions. Others push past it. 120°. 150°. 180°.

These are the bearings that don't explain themselves to visitors. They're for the person who made the map.

## How to Find Your Bearing

The fastest path is to open the editor, zoom to your neighbourhood, and drag the bearing control slowly clockwise from 0°.

You'll feel a click — a point where the streets suddenly look right. Not "geographically correct." Right, as in: *this is how I remember it.*

That's your bearing.

:::tip Find your neighbourhood's natural angle
Most streets aren't built at exactly 0° or 45° — they follow topography, coastline, or the decision of one 19th-century planner. Try 15°, 22°, or 37°. The map will tell you when you've hit it.
:::

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I change the bearing after I've ordered?**
Not after the order is placed, but the editor lets you experiment as much as you want before you commit. There's no cost to adjusting the bearing 50 times.

**Does bearing change the price?**
No — all bearings are included in the same base price.

**Which bearings are most popular?**
0° and 30° account for the majority of orders. But the most interesting prints we see are usually in the 22°–60° range, where the city geometry finds a non-obvious visual logic.

**Does bearing work differently for round cities vs grid cities?**
Grid cities (Medellín downtown, Buenos Aires, Montevideo centro) respond dramatically to bearing changes. Organic cities — older European cores, some Asian and Latin American colonial centres — tend to be more stable across bearings because the streets themselves are non-orthogonal.
