## The Prints That Resonate for Mexican Expats

**Mexico City (CDMX)**
At *colonia* level, CDMX is one of the most cartographically rich cities in the world. La Condesa's circular park-ringed streets, Roma Norte's grid-within-diagonals, Coyoacán's colonial twists, Polanco's tree-lined regularity — each *colonia* has a visual fingerprint that disappears at city-wide scale. Zoom in. Centre it on your corner. Try 30° bearing and watch the Avenida geometry shift.

**Guadalajara**
Mexico's second city has one of the cleanest colonial grids in Latin America. The historic centre, Zapopan, Tlaquepaque — each has a different urban texture. At medium zoom, the *callejones* and plazas of the centro histórico become a study in pre-industrial planning. Warm palettes suit Guadalajara's golden stone.

**Oaxaca**
Oaxaca city is small enough that a single print can capture the entire centro histórico and its surrounding *barrios* — Santo Tomás Xochimilco, Jalatlaco, Xochimilco. The street names alone are visual art. For someone from Oaxaca living abroad, a map of their city is a map of their language, their market, their home.

**San Miguel de Allende**
The cobblestone grid of SMA's historic centre — with the Parroquia at its heart — is one of the most recognizable urban footprints in Mexico. Zoom to neighbourhood level and the texture of a colonial city built before cars becomes apparent. Vintage Noir works beautifully here; the contrast suits stone and shadow.

**Monterrey**
The industrial north has its own cartographic character. The Barrio Antiguo, San Pedro Garza García, the Sierra Madre framing the east — Monterrey seen at neighbourhood scale shows a city built around geography and commerce, not colonial planning. A different Mexico, equally worth printing.

**The Pueblo**
The town in Oaxaca, Chiapas, Hidalgo, Michoacán — the one your family is from. The central *zócalo*, the streets that radiate from the church, the *milpa* roads at the edge. MapVibe prints it. Probably no one else does.
